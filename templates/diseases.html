Diabetic Retinopathy DR microvascular complication of diabetes mellitus and is a leading cause of vision loss in the elderly and working population. The image is labeled as DR if it shows any of the following clinical findings: microaneurysms, retinal dot and blot hemorrhage, hard exudates or cotton wool spots.
Age-Related Macular Degeneration ARMD Progressive chronic disease of the central retina that usually develops in old age people and is a leading cause of vision loss. The image is labeled as ARMD if it shows any of the following clinical findings: multiple drusen in macular region, geographic atrophy involving the fovea, features of neovascular age-related macular degeneration
Media Haze MH The opacity of media can be a hallmark for the presence of cataracts, vitreous opacities, corneal edema or small pupils. Moreover, some other artifacts may be introduced as a result of acquisition procedures, such as eyelash artifacts and artifacts introduced by the instrument.
Drusens DN Drusens are yellow or white extracellular deposits located between the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) and Bruch's membrane. They naturally occur in the aged population. The presence of drusen is a hallmark and early sign of significant risk of age-related macular degeneration, geographic atrophy, choroidal neovascularization, and development of RPE abnormalities
Myopia MYA Myopia is characterized by degenerative changes in the choroid, sclera, and RPE. Vision loss due to myopia can be progressive and irreversible.
Branch retinal vein occlusion BRVO Branch retinal vein occlusion is the most common visual disorder causing visual blurring in the peripheral region. The visual signs in the retina can be any of the following: occlusion of one or more branches of the central retinal vein, dot and blot hemorrhage, flame-shaped hemorrhage, soft and hard exudates, retinal edema, and dilated, tortuous vein.
Tessellation TSLN Tessellation in the fundus images appears due to the thinning of the RPE and choriocapillaris. The choroidal vessels are visible due to the reduced density of the pigments. Mild tessellation occurs with aging and it might be present in normal retinal image. Moderate tessellations are related to myopia.
Epiretinal membrane ERM Epiretinal membrane is a thin sheet of glial cells that appears as an irregular light reflex on the surface of macular region of the retina. Symptoms associated with ERM are loss of central vision and decreased visual acuity.
Laser scars LS Laser scars indicate that patient had underwent laser therapy which is predominately preferred treatment method to stop the progression of vascular leaks. This treatment results in circular or irregular shaped scars on the retinal surface.
Macular scar MS Macular scar is either caused by infection or inflammatory intraocular reaction that causes slight injury to RPE. It is responsible for complications or decreased vision.
Central serous retinopathy CSR Central serous retinopathy is characterized as a round serous detachment of the neurosensory retina from the underlying RPE. The subretinal fluid is a clinical indicator for the presence of CSR. The vision loss depends on the amount of detachment of neurosensory retina.
Optic disc cupping ODC Optic disc cupping is the thinning of neuroretinal rim such that optic disc appears excavated. Pathological ODC is generally referred to as glaucoma. However, several other non-glaucomatous diseases, such as arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy and central retinal vein occlusion, also result in ODC.
Central retinal vein occlusion CRVO Central retinal vein occlusion is the occlusion of central retinal vein with a start point at or proximal to the lamina cribrosa of the optic nerve. The clinical signs are presence of flame-shaped hemorrhages. It may develop permanent, severe, visual impairment.
Tortuous vessels TV Tortuous vessels is characterized by pattern of marked tortuosity of the retinal blood vessels. It is associated with diseases, like hypertension, diabetes, and other genetic disorders.
Asteroid hyalosis AH Asteroid hyalosis is a condition in which numerous astroid bodies are dispersed in vitreous. The presence of AH may be associated with diabetes, hyperlipidemia, or hypertension.
Optic disc pallor ODP Optic disc pallor refers to pale yellow discoloration of the optic disc, as well as absence of many small vessels. Vision loss due to ODP depends on the degree of pallor.
Optic disc edema ODE Optic disc edema includes findings, such as papilledema, papillitis, and choked or swollen discs. It is a classic marker that represent initial signs of anterior ischemic optic neuropathy, this may eventually lead to loss of vision.
Optociliary shunt ST Optociliary shunt vessels are vessels on the optic nerve head that connect the retinal venous system to the choroid. The clinical findings are presence of prepapillary vascular loops or optociliary shunt vessels. It causes slowly progressive vision loss.
Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy AION Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy is caused by the damage of anterior part of optic nerve resulting in ischemia. In middle-aged and elderly people, it is one of the leading cause of seriously impaired vision or blindness.
Parafoveal telangiectasia PT Parafoveal telangiectasia is a clinical condition characterized by yellow, lipid-rich exudation or parafoveal graying or abnormalities from distention and tortuous blood vessels and incompetent retinal capillaries in the juxtafoveolar region. It is responsible for central vision loss.
Retinal traction RT Retinal traction detachment is separation of the neurosensory retina from the RPE due to traction. Under the label RT, images showing presence of traction and retinal traction detachment are included.
Retinitis RS Retinitis is characterized by the presence of vitreous inflammation, macular star, intraretinal hemorrhage, phlebitis, arteritis, and hyperemic disc. The most common visual loss in this case is central vision loss.
Chorioretinitis CRS Chorioretinitis causes inflammation of choroid and retina which results in vision-threatening complications. It is usually caused by infections, like toxoplasmosis and cytomegalovirus.
Exudation EDN Exudation: The hard exudates are white or yellowish lipid deposits with sharp edges. Circinate retinopathy is a clinical condition marked by a circle of exudates surrounding the macular area. Exudative maculopathy is a clinical condition marked by masses exudates in the macular region. Macular star is a star-like formation of exudates in the macular region. All these conditions are labeled under category EDN.
Retinal pigment epithelium changes RPEC Retinal pigment epithelium changes are characterized by the structural changes, such as increased thickness of Bruch's membrane, loss of melanin granules, accumulation of lipofuscin, formation of drusen, increase in the density of residual bodies, accumulation of basal deposits on or within Bruch’s membrane, and disorganization of the basal infoldings and microvilli atrophy. These changes lead to severe visual loss.
Macular hole MHL Macular hole is a small retinal break located in the center of the fovea, causing significant vision impairment.
Retinitis pigmentosa RP Retinitis pigmentosa, retina progressively degenerates. The visual signs are bone-spicule deposits and arterial narrowing. It causes night blindness, which eventually leads to complete blindness.
Cotton-wool spots CWS Cotton-wool spots are whitish, fluffy patches appearing on the surface of retina. They are manifestations of common retinal diseases, like diabetes mellitus, acquired immunodeficiency syndrome, and systemic hypertension.
Coloboma CB Coloboma is marked as a missing portion of tissue in both the choroid and retina at the time of birth. It can be visualized as a white zone of the retinal fundus images. It is responsible for the loss of visual fields, cataract and retinal detachment may occur.
Optic disc pit maculopathy ODPM Optic disc pit is a rare unilateral and sporadic congenital abnormality of the optic disc. ODPM is characterized by intraretinal and subretinal fluid at the macula, causing progressive visual loss.
Preretinal hemorrhage PRH Preretinal hemorrhage usually appears as a boat-shaped hemorrhage which obscures the underlying retina. Underlying causes include bleeding from neovascularization, vascular occlusion, hypertensive retinopathy, retinal tear, posterior vitreous detachment and rupture of the internal limiting membrane.
Myelinated nerve fibers MNF Myelinated nerve fibers are inborn irregularities that are characterized as gray-white opaque lesions with feathery edges. They may be located along nerve fibers of the optic disc or elsewhere on the retina. It is associated with diseases, like pituitary tumor, embolism of the central retinal, multiple sclerosis, and optic nerve atrophy.
Hemorrhagic retinopathy HR Hemorrhagic retinopathy is a particular form of retinopathy which is hemorrhagic in nature. It is associated with diabetes, hypertension, and occlusion of the central vein.
Central retinal artery occlusion CRAO Central retinal artery occlusion occurs due to embolus blocking the central retinal artery. It is characterized by pale, whitening, and retinal swelling. It is responsible for sudden, catastrophic visual loss.
Tilted disc TD Tilted disc is tilting of the optic disc and usually occurs bilaterally. It is associated with high myopia.
Cystoid macular edema CME Cystoid macular edema is characterized by the multiple cyst-like (cystoid) areas in the macula and causes retinal edema (swelling). It causes decreased or blurred central vision.
Post-traumatic choroidal rupture PTCR Post-traumatic choroidal rupture are breaks in the choroid, Bruch's membrane, and RPE which occurs as an effect of blunt ocular trauma. It leads to serous macular or haemorrhagic detachment.
Choroidal folds CF Choroidal folds is a series of dark and light lines usually temporal and confined to the posterior pole. They occur after some surgical procedures and in a variety of ocular diseases.
Vitreous hemorrhage VH Vitreous hemorrhage is extravasated blood in one of the spaces created around the vitreous body. It is a result of neovascularization or retinal tears or blood vessel bleeding.
Macroaneurysm MCA Macroaneurysm is fusiform or round dilation of the retinal arterioles which occur in the temporal retina. It is generally unilateral and surrounded by circinate exudation. The central vision loss is due to the presence of edema, hemorrhage, and exudation.
Vasculitis VS Vasculitis is inflammation of retinal blood vessels. It is characterized by white sheathing due to the presence of exudates around retinal blood vessels or cuffing of the affected vessels. It causes exudation, retinal swelling, and macular edema.
Branch retinal artery occlusion BRAO Branch retinal artery occlusion occurs due to acute retinal artery obstructions. It may cause sectoral visual field loss.
Plaque PLQ Plaque is bright orange-yellow in color and are located in retinal arteries. If the plaque is present in retina then refer the patient for investigation of the cardiovascular system.
Hemorrhagic pigment epithelial detachment HPED Hemorrhagic pigment epithelial detachment the pigment epithelium is separated due to hemorrhage from the Bruch's membrane. It is a predictive feature for the presence of polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy or choroidal neovascularization.
Collateral CL Collateral is new retinal vessels developed within the framework of existing vessel network. It is a hallmark for the disease, like optic disc drusen, high myopia, and diabetes.
